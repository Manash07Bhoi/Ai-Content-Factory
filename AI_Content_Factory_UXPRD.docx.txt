1. Executive Summary & UX Vision
AI Content Factory is a dual-surface platform: a high-throughput content operations tool for internal staff, and a premium digital product marketplace for creative professionals. The UX design must simultaneously feel like an enterprise command centre — powerful, dense with information, always in control — and like a luxury creative marketplace — aspirational, tactile, effortlessly beautiful. These two surfaces share a design system but express it differently: the admin surface is dark, data-rich, and operationally focused; the marketplace is cinematic, product-centred, and conversion-optimised.
The emotional promise: Every person who touches this product — whether an admin triggering a generation batch at 2am or a designer browsing prompt packs on mobile — should feel like they are operating the most sophisticated tool in their category. Nothing should feel accidental. Every interaction should feel inevitable.

2. Design System Foundations
The design system is token-first. Every value used in the UI — colour, spacing, radius, shadow, motion duration — is a named token. This ensures perfect consistency across the React component library, makes theme switching (dark ↔ light) a single CSS variable swap, and gives engineers a single source of truth.

2.6 Motion & Animation System

4. Admin Surface — Screen Specifications
The admin surface operates on a persistent dark theme with the page background at --color-surface-0 (#0F0F1A). A collapsible sidebar (260px expanded, 72px collapsed) anchors navigation. A sticky top bar (56px) holds global search, notifications, and the user menu. The content area fills the remaining viewport and scrolls independently from the sidebar and top bar.


Screen A1 — Login / Authentication
Layout
Full-viewport split: left 50% is a cinematic background — a dark gradient with slowly animating noise texture and floating abstract particles representing the content generation pipeline (subtle, non-distracting, 0.3 opacity). Right 50% is the auth card centred in --color-surface-1, with generous padding. On mobile (< 768px), the left panel collapses entirely and the auth card fills the full screen with a compact brand mark at top.
Visual Hierarchy
* Top: Small brand logo (32px height) + wordmark in Inter weight-700.
* H1: 'Welcome back' (text-3xl, brand-gradient text — CSS background-clip). Subtitle: 'Sign in to your AI Content Factory account'.
* Form: Email input (label floats on focus), Password input (show/hide toggle icon), 'Remember me' checkbox, 'Forgot password?' link right-aligned.
* Primary CTA: 'Sign in' button full-width, height 48px (lg size), brand-700 background.
* Divider + 'or continue with' (future OAuth placeholder, grayed out with 'Coming soon' tooltip).
* Footer: 'Don't have an account? Browse as guest' for customer surface link.
2FA Screen (Step 2)
Same layout. Auth card replaces with: '2-Step Verification' heading, compact inline description, six individual digit inputs (24px each, spaced, auto-focus-advance on each keystroke), paste detection (auto-fill all 6 from clipboard), 30-second countdown ring for TOTP expiry, 'Use backup code' link below, 'Back to login' ghost link.
States
State
	Visual Treatment
	Loading (submit)
	Button replaces text with spinner + 'Signing in…', all inputs disabled, cursor-wait
	Error
	Red banner above form fades in: 'Invalid email or password. X attempts remaining.' Input borders turn danger.
	Success
	Button turns success-green with checkmark, brief 400ms hold, then smooth page transition to dashboard
	2FA Invalid
	Digit inputs shake (keyframe), turn danger-border, auto-clear after 1s, focus returns to first digit
	Account Locked
	Banner: 'Account temporarily locked. Try again in 14 minutes.' with countdown timer.
	Mobile Adaptations
* Auth card: full-width, no horizontal padding beyond 16px safe area inset.
* Inputs: 48px height minimum for touch target compliance (WCAG 2.5.5).
* Password show/hide: 44×44px touch target.
* 'Sign in' button: fixed to bottom of viewport with 8px padding on very small screens (<400px).

6. Micro-Interactions & Motion Bible
Every animation in the system has a reason to exist. The following eight signature interactions define the motion character of the product. Engineers should implement these exactly as specified — they collectively create the sense that the interface is alive and responsive.


MI-01 — The Generation Counter
Trigger
A new AI generation batch completes and the pending review count increments in the KPI card and the sidebar badge.
Animation Sequence
1. Sidebar badge: scale(1.0) → scale(1.35) in 150ms (spring, stiffness 400, damping 10)
2. Sidebar badge: new number cross-fades in during scale peak (75ms opacity 0→1)
3. Sidebar badge: scale(1.35) → scale(1.0) in 200ms (ease-out)
4. KPI card value: starts counting up from previous value to new value
   Duration: min(800ms, countDelta × 20ms) capped at 1200ms
   Easing: cubic-bezier(0, 0, 0.2, 1) — fast start, gentle finish
5. KPI card: brief brand-glow pulse (shadow 0→glow→0 over 600ms)
Reduced Motion Alternative
Badge number updates instantly. KPI value jumps to new value with a single 150ms opacity cross-fade (no transform, no count-up).


MI-02 — The Review Approval
Trigger
Reviewer clicks Approve (or presses A).
Animation Sequence
1. Approve button: scale(0.95) in 50ms (instant press feedback)
2. Button background transitions from brand-700 to success (150ms ease-in)
3. Button text cross-fades to '✓ Approved' in 100ms
4. Content item in left list: green left border slides in from left (200ms ease-out)
5. Status badge in list item: morphs from 'Pending' amber to 'Approved' green (200ms)
6. After 300ms: next item in queue auto-loads into preview panel
   — translateX(20px, opacity 0) → translateX(0, opacity 1) over 250ms ease-out
7. Progress counter at list top increments (same as MI-01 badge animation)


MI-03 — The Product Pack Generation
Trigger
Admin clicks 'Generate Pack' in Product Builder Step 3.
Animation Sequence
1. Button collapses to a pill (width: 100% → 48px, 300ms spring)
   Pill shows indeterminate circular progress spinner
2. Stage indicators animate in one by one (stagger 150ms per stage):
   Each stage: translateY(8px, opacity 0) → translateY(0, opacity 1)
3. Per stage, the stage icon animates:
   - 'Creating PDF…': document pages fan out (CSS transform stagger)
   - 'Bundling…': box closes and seals (CSS keyframe)
   - 'Uploading…': upward arrow bounces (translateY loop)
   - 'Finalising…': checkmarks appear sequentially
4. On complete: all stages get checkmarks (stagger 80ms each)
5. Success card slides up from bottom: translateY(40px, opacity 0) → normal (400ms spring)


MI-04 — The Cart Add & Checkout Celebration
Trigger
Customer clicks 'Buy Now' on a product and the order succeeds.
Animation Sequence
Checkout success animation:
1. Background gradient sweeps in (0% → 100% coverage, 700ms ease-out)
2. Success circle SVG: strokeDashoffset 314px → 0 over 600ms ease-in-out
3. Checkmark SVG: strokeDashoffset 48px → 0 over 400ms ease-out (starts 400ms after circle)
4. Confetti burst: 60 particles spawn at checkmark centre
   - Random velocities (spread 360°), initial speed 8–14px/frame
   - Gravity: +0.3px/frame²
   - Each particle: random size (6–14px), random rotation speed
   - Colour: brand-500, accent, success, warning (equal probability)
   - Lifetime: 2000ms, opacity fades from 1→0 in final 500ms
5. Heading: 'Your pack is ready!' slides up from +20px over 500ms (starts 800ms after circle)
6. Download buttons stagger in: each +100ms delay, same slide-up


MI-05 — The Quality Score Reveal
Trigger
A content item is opened in the review queue preview panel.
Animation Sequence
1. Quality score bar: width animates from 0% to actual% over 600ms
   Easing: cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1) — spring overshoot for visual interest
   Colour: transitions through red → amber → green based on score (CSS hue interpolation)
2. Score number: count-up from 0 to actual score, same 600ms duration
3. Sub-score bars (if expanded): stagger 80ms apart, same animation per bar
4. Score badge: scale(0) → scale(1) with spring at animation end (200ms)


MI-06 — The Sidebar Collapse
Trigger
Admin clicks the hamburger/collapse button to toggle the sidebar.
Animation Sequence
Collapse (260px → 72px):
1. Nav item labels: opacity 1→0 in 150ms
2. Nav item labels: visibility hidden after 150ms
3. Sidebar width: 260px → 72px over 250ms cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)
4. Hamburger icon: 3 bars morph to stacked-dots icon over 200ms
5. Main content area: margin-left adjusts simultaneously (no content jump)


Expand (72px → 260px):
1. Width: 72px → 260px over 250ms (same easing)
2. Nav item labels: visibility visible immediately
3. Labels: opacity 0→1 starting at 100ms into width animation
4. Icon reverts to hamburger bars


MI-07 — The Live Feed Item Arrival
Trigger
A new event arrives via Server-Sent Events and is prepended to the activity feed.
Animation Sequence
1. New item renders above the list at height: 0, opacity: 0, overflow: hidden
2. Height animates from 0 to item's natural height over 300ms ease-out
3. Opacity: 0 → 1 from 100ms to 300ms
4. A subtle brand-coloured flash (bg-brand-900 → transparent) highlights the new row
   over 1000ms ease-out — marking it as 'new' without being distracting
5. If feed exceeds max display count (25 items), last item slides out:
   height: natural → 0, opacity: 1 → 0 over 200ms ease-in


MI-08 — The Page Transition
Trigger
User navigates between major sections (via React Router).
Animation Sequence
Outgoing page:
1. opacity: 1 → 0, translateX: 0 → -20px over 150ms ease-in


Incoming page:
1. Starts at: opacity: 0, translateX: 20px
2. Transitions to: opacity: 1, translateX: 0 over 250ms ease-out
3. Delay: 50ms after outgoing animation starts (overlap for continuity)


For drill-down navigations (e.g., order list → order detail):
- Outgoing: scale(1.0) → scale(0.97), opacity: 1→0, 200ms
- Incoming: scale(1.02) → scale(1.0), opacity: 0→1, 300ms


Loading skeleton during route transition:
- Skeleton screens use a shimmer animation: linear-gradient sweeps L→R
  over 1500ms, infinite, with 300ms delay between loops

8. Edge Cases & Resilience Design
A design system is only as good as its handling of failure states. The following specifications ensure the platform never looks broken, even when something is.


8.1 Loading States — Skeleton Screens
Every data-driven component has a skeleton screen that matches its exact layout. Skeletons use a shimmer animation (150ms → 1500ms loop). The rule is: skeletons must be shown within 100ms of a navigation event. Never show a blank white/dark screen.
Component
	Skeleton Anatomy
	KPI Card
	Rect placeholder for icon circle (48px). Two rect lines for value (40px height) and label (16px height). Small rect for trend pill. Full card height matches real card.
	Product Card
	Full-width rect (aspect ratio 4:3) for thumbnail. 3 rect lines below for title, description, and price row. All with border-radius matching component.
	Data Table
	Header row: 5 rect cells matching column widths. 8 body rows: same structure as header. Alternating row shading preserved.
	Chart
	Chart container has a central loading state: circular spinner (brand-coloured) + 'Loading data…' text. No skeleton bars (misleading for charts).
	Review Preview
	Left panel: 8 stacked text lines at varying widths (60%/80%/70%/90%/60%/40%/70%/80%). Right panel (poster): large rect placeholder.
	

8.2 Empty States
Screen/Component
	Empty State Design
	Review Queue (no pending items)
	Centred illustration: a serene mountain landscape with 'All clear' text (visual metaphor for calm). Heading: 'Queue is empty — great work!'. Subtext: 'Next generation batch runs in X hours.' [countdown timer]. CTA: 'Trigger generation now' button. Subtle confetti if this is the first time all items were reviewed.
	Marketplace (no products)
	Centred abstract generative art SVG. 'Coming Soon' heading. 'Products are being created right now.' Subtext. Animated progress bar showing generation progress (live from API). Email notification signup field.
	Order History (no orders)
	Shopping bag illustration with sparkles. 'No purchases yet'. 'Explore our digital packs' CTA button. 3 featured product cards below as inspiration.
	Wishlist (empty)
	Heart outline illustration. 'Your wishlist is empty'. 'Save products you love by clicking the heart icon.' Browse button.
	Search (zero results)
	Magnifying glass with a question mark. 'No results for [query]'. Suggestions section. Trending searches.
	Notifications (none)
	Bell illustration. 'You're all caught up!'. 'We'll notify you about orders, price drops, and new releases.' Preferences link.
	Analytics (new account, no data)
	Chart illustration with upward trend (aspirational). 'Your analytics will appear here once you start selling.' Setup checklist of 3 steps.
	

8.3 Error States
Error Type
	Design Response
	API timeout / 500 error
	Inline error state in the affected component only (not full-page). Error card with: warning icon, 'Something went wrong' heading, technical message (dev only), 'Try again' button that retries the request with exponential backoff. Toast notification: 'Failed to load data. Retrying…'
	Network offline
	Global banner (top, amber): 'You appear to be offline. Some features may be unavailable.' Dismissible. Auto-dismisses on reconnect with a 'Back online' success toast. Cached data remains visible with a subtle 'Showing cached data from X mins ago' label.
	403 Forbidden
	Graceful redirect to the most appropriate accessible page with a toast: 'You don't have permission to view that.' Not a full error page — stay in context.
	404 Not Found
	A dedicated 404 page with brand illustration, 'Page not found' heading, search bar to find what they were looking for, and home/marketplace navigation buttons. The URL is preserved so the user can see what they tried to visit.
	Form validation error
	Field-level: inline error message below field, field border turns danger. Multi-field: a summary error banner above the form listing all errors. Scroll to first error automatically on form submission attempt.
	Payment failure (Stripe)
	Inline: Stripe error message (never obscured or replaced with a generic message — Stripe messages are already user-friendly). Card field highlighted. 'Learn more' link to payment troubleshooting. Support chat trigger.
	LLM generation failure
	Generation card shows error state: 'Generation failed for X/50 items. Y items were created successfully.' Partial results preserved. 'Retry failed items' button requeues only the failed items.
	

8.4 Performance Edge Cases
Scenario
	Design Response
	Very long prompt text (1000+ chars)
	Text truncates at 3 lines with a 'Show more' expand button. Full text visible in detail panel/preview only. Never causes layout overflow.
	1000+ items in review queue
	Virtual list rendering (react-window or @tanstack/virtual). Only visible rows are in the DOM. Scroll position preserved on item update. 'Jump to top' FAB appears after scrolling 500px.
	Slow image loading (posters)
	Placeholder: brand-coloured blurred gradient matching the poster's dominant colour (extracted server-side, stored as hex). Progressive JPEG loading: thumbnail first, then full resolution swaps in.
	Large data table (1000+ rows)
	Pagination (20 rows/page) as primary pattern. Optional: virtualized infinite scroll with 'X of Y rows loaded' counter. Column-level filter before fetch (reduces payload).
	Slow generation batch (100+ items)
	Progress is chunked: 'X of 100 generated' updates every 5 items. Cancel button always available. Partial results visible in review queue in real-time as items complete.

9.2 Focus Management
* All interactive elements have a visible focus ring: 2px solid --border-focus (brand-500) with 2px offset (not outline:none anywhere).
* Focus rings use box-shadow: 0 0 0 2px var(--bg-page), 0 0 0 4px var(--border-focus) to create a gap-effect that works on any background.
* Modal dialogs: on open, focus moves to the first interactive element inside the modal. On close, focus returns to the trigger element.
* Dropdown menus: arrow keys navigate between options. Type-to-filter on multi-selects. Home/End keys jump to first/last item.
* The review queue keyboard shortcuts (A/R/E/←/→) only activate when the focus is within the review panel — not globally (to prevent conflicts with browser shortcuts and form inputs).
* Skip navigation link: 'Skip to main content' is the first focusable element on every page, visible only on focus (opacity 0 → 1 on :focus). Links to the main landmark.

10. Dark / Light Mode Implementation
Dark mode is the primary theme for the admin surface. Light mode is the primary theme for the customer marketplace. Both surfaces support the opposite theme, with preferences saved per-user. System preference (prefers-color-scheme) is the default on first visit.


10.1 Theme Architecture
/* Root CSS Variables — Light Mode (default) */
:root {
  --bg-page:       #F9FAFB;
  --bg-card:       #FFFFFF;
  --text-primary:  #111827;
  --border-default:#E5E7EB;
  /* ... all 40 semantic tokens */
}


/* Dark Mode Override */
:root[data-theme='dark'],
@media (prefers-color-scheme: dark) {
  :root:not([data-theme='light']) {
    --bg-page:       #0F0F1A;
    --bg-card:       #161625;
    --text-primary:  #F9FAFB;
    --border-default:#312E81;
    /* ... dark overrides for all 40 tokens */
  }
}


10.2 Theme Toggle Interaction
The theme toggle button (sun/moon icon in top bar and account settings) uses a morphing icon animation: the sun rays retract inward (stroke-width 2→0, 200ms) as the moon shape rotates in (rotate -90deg → 0, 250ms). The page background cross-fades between themes over 300ms using CSS transition on the :root variables. No flash on load — the script that reads and applies the saved theme preference runs in the <head> (before paint) to prevent flash.

	Glassmorphic panels

	Code/mono text

12. Design Rationale — Why This Design Works
The following explains the reasoning behind the most significant design decisions — not what was chosen, but why it will perform better commercially and operationally than the alternatives.


12.1 Dark-First Admin Surface
AI content operations run around the clock. Reviewers spending 4+ hours at a screen need a low-fatigue environment. Dark mode reduces luminance significantly which reduces the accommodative demand of the eye — this is clinically measurable. The deep violet-indigo palette was specifically chosen (not pure grey/black) because violet sits between warm and cool in the spectrum, avoiding the clinical coldness of blue-grey admin tools and the visual fatigue of warm-toned dark UIs. The brand glow effect (brand-coloured shadows on active elements) creates a neon-on-dark aesthetic that is simultaneously futuristic and premium without being garish.


12.2 The Dual-Panel Review Interface
The most critical bottleneck in the business model is the review throughput. The dual-panel design with keyboard shortcuts is not a nice-to-have — it is a commercial multiplier. A reviewer who uses keyboard shortcuts (A/R/→) can process an item in approximately 8 seconds versus 18 seconds using mouse-only interaction. With 200 items per day, this is a 33-minute saving per reviewer per day. The keyboard shortcut hints shown in the action bar are always visible (not hidden in a help modal) because help that requires a discovery step rarely gets used.


12.3 The Counter Animation on First Load
The count-up animation on dashboard KPI cards when the page first loads is not decoration. User research consistently shows that animated numbers are perceived as more credible than static numbers — the animation implies that the number was 'calculated' rather than simply retrieved. It also gives the user 800ms to orient themselves on the dashboard before processing the final values. This maps to how financial dashboards like Bloomberg Terminal handle heavy data loads — motion creates a sense of computation, and computation implies accuracy.


12.4 Why the Marketplace Uses Light Mode
Customer-facing e-commerce platforms that use dark-mode-primary have measurably lower conversion rates in A/B tests across most product categories. The reason is trust and price perception: light mode is associated with transparency and openness. Dark mode in commerce contexts is associated with luxury (like a dark boutique store) which works for very high-end products but raises the price expectation significantly. For a digital marketplace with products ranging from $4.99 to $49.99, the light mode creates a more approachable 'good value' perception. The brand violet accent on white is highly legible and creates strong purchase CTA contrast.


12.5 The Sticky Purchase Panel
The purchase panel on product detail pages becomes sticky on desktop and tablet because the decision to buy can happen at any point while reading about the product — after the description, after reading reviews, after examining the 'What's Included' section. A sticky panel means the purchase friction (scrolling back to top to click 'Buy') is eliminated at every decision point. On mobile, the sticky bottom bar serves the same function with even more emphasis — it is always visible, always one tap away.


12.6 Variable Font Weight on KPI Values
KPI card values use font-weight: 550 (mid-variable between 500 and 600) instead of standard 600 bold. This subtle difference makes the numbers feel precise and technical rather than brash and heavy. It is the visual equivalent of a Bloomberg data terminal versus a newspaper headline. Inter Variable's optical size axis also means that at the 30px display size used for KPI values, the letterforms are slightly wider and more legible than the standard 600-weight specimen would suggest.


12.7 Confetti on Purchase Success
Purchase success confetti is not a gimmick. It is a proven mechanism for creating a 'shareable moment' — users who experience genuine delight at purchase completion are measurably more likely to share their purchase on social media and return for future purchases. The confetti implementation is deliberately lightweight (canvas-based, 60 particles, 2-second lifetime) and gracefully omits itself when prefers-reduced-motion is set. It fires only once per order — repeat views of the success page do not trigger it.
